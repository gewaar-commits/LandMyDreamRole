Cover Letter Prompt
A cover letter has one job: make someone want to have the conversation. It doesn't summarize your resume. It doesn't perform enthusiasm. It shows why this role fits the way you work — in your voice, with enough specificity that it couldn't have been written by anyone else.

Side note - I really, really prefer not to do cover letters. They never feel great. I also usually rewrite the cover letter that is generated for me. This one needs a lot of work, style guide tweaking, etc. But, seeing the draft is a good start.


The prompt
Please write a cover letter for the following role. 

**Company:** [company name]

**Role:** [exact job title]

**Hiring manager name (if known):** [name]

**What I know about the company or role beyond the JD:** [referral context, recent news, why it caught your eye, any concerns]

**Job description:**

[paste full JD]

**My Career Memory Bank:**

[paste or attach]

**My current resume:**

[paste or attach]

---

Before writing, do the following:

### Step 1 — Decode the role

In 3-4 sentences, tell me what this role is actually asking for beneath the JD language. What type of leader, what environment, what's the real problem they're hiring to solve? I want to confirm your read before you write.

### Step 2 — Identify the one or two threads

From my Career Memory Bank and resume, what are the one or two experiences that map most directly to what this role needs — not the most impressive things on paper, but the most relevant to this specific problem? These become the backbone of the letter.

### Step 3 — Write the letter

**Guidelines:**

- 3 short paragraphs, 200-250 words total. No longer.

- Do not restate the resume. The reader has it.

- Do not open with "I am excited to apply." Find a real entry point — what draws me to this specific role or company, in one concrete sentence.

- Use one or two specific proof points. Not a list. One thread, developed briefly.

- Match the company's tone without overperforming it. If they're direct and unpretentious, be direct. If they're warmer, be warmer. Don't inflate.

- End with energy, not formality. Something that leans forward.

- Sound like me — warm, direct, no corporate jargon. If it could have been written by anyone, rewrite it.

- No em-dashes.

**Structure:**

- Para 1 (~50 words): Why this role, why now. Specific — not generic enthusiasm.

- Para 2 (~120 words): The one or two threads. What I've done that's directly relevant, briefly and concretely. One metric or outcome if it earns its place.

- Para 3 (~50 words): What I'm looking to bring, and a forward-leaning close.

### Step 4 — Flag anything you inferred

After the letter, note: anything you assumed about the role or company that I should verify before sending, any place you made a judgment call about what to include or leave out, any line that risks sounding like someone else.


What makes a cover letter work
Specificity over enthusiasm. "I'm excited about [company]'s position at the intersection of marketing and revenue" tells them nothing. "What draws me to this role is the chance to help build a sales technology foundation that can supercharge the next stage of growth" — still short, but it signals you understand what the role is for.

One real thread, not a highlights reel. The instinct is to pack in everything relevant. Resist it. One experience developed briefly is more persuasive than four experiences mentioned.

Their tone, not yours. Read the JD and any public writing from the company. Match the register — not the vocabulary, the register. A startup that writes informally doesn't want formal. A company that writes carefully doesn't want breezy.

A close that leans forward. "I look forward to hearing from you" is a cover letter fading out. "I'd love to bring this energy and momentum to [company]" is a cover letter leaning in. The difference is small but it matters.

### 1. Company snapshot
- What they do, who they serve, current market position
- Stage, size, recent news (funding, layoffs, product launches, M&A, leadership changes)
- Culture signals from Glassdoor, leadership quotes, published values — and what they actually suggest about how the place runs
- Why this role likely exists now (growth, replacement, new function, reorg)
- Their tech stack, especially any overlap with my background

### 2. Role analysis — what this job actually is
Read past the JD language. Decode:
- What this role owns day-to-day vs. what the JD says
- The real problems they're hiring to solve
- Who this person reports to and what that leader cares about
- What success looks like at 30 / 90 / 180 days
- Red flags or ambiguities in the JD worth probing in the interview
- Where this role sits in the org and what that implies about influence vs. authority

### 3. JD line-by-line breakdown
Take each requirement in the JD — stated and implied — and map it to one of four categories:
- **Strong match:** I have direct experience, it's on my resume, I can defend it
- **Present but buried:** Real experience that isn't visible in my current materials — surface it
- **Analogous:** Adjacent experience that needs a bridge — note what the bridge is
- **Genuine gap:** I don't have this or my exposure is too thin to claim it

For each genuine gap: what's the honest framing, what's the reframe, and what's the specific line I'd use if asked directly?

Present this as a table:

| JD Requirement | Category | My Proof Point or Gap Response |
|---|---|---|

### 4. Stakeholder intelligence
For each known interviewer, research and provide:
- Career background and trajectory (where they came from, how they got here)
- Communication style signals — from LinkedIn posts, articles, talks, how they write, what they champion
- What they're likely optimizing for in this hire — what problem are they trying to solve by bringing someone in?
- What will land well with them specifically, and what to avoid
- Any shared context, common ground, or rapport-builders
- How to adapt the pitch and tone for this person vs. a generic version

If interviewers are unknown: build a likely stakeholder map based on the role level and company type. Who is probably in the room, what does each care about, how do they differ?

Note: hiring manager research helps you connect more easily — which verbs to lead with, which proof points to surface, which parts of the background to emphasize.

### 5. Strengths alignment
3-5 specific strengths mapped directly to this role's requirements. Format each as:

**[What the role needs]** → **[Specific proof point with outcome]**

Lead with the integrator narrative where it fits. Include metrics, scope signals, and outcomes. No generic claims — if it could appear on 100 other resumes, rewrite it.

### 6. Gaps — honest assessment
2-4 real gaps. Don't soft-pedal. For each:
- The gap (title mismatch, tool experience, industry, scope)
- The reframe — how to address it proactively and confidently without overclaiming
- The line — a specific, natural-sounding response I could actually say

### 7. ATEL pitch — tailored for this role and interviewer
Build a 90-second spoken pitch using the ATEL structure:

- **Anchor (~10 sec):** One sentence positioning. Lead with integrator identity.
- **Origin (~15 sec):** Brief origin — CS background, café owner, enterprise systems architect. One sentence that shows this has always been who I am.
- **Experience ladder (~50 sec):** 3 escalating moments building toward this specific role. 1-2 sentences each. End at the most recent relevant achievement.
- **Loop (~15 sec):** "What I'm looking for next is..." tied specifically to this company's stated challenge or mission.

Provide:
1. Primary version (for the hiring manager)
2. Alternative version (for a recruiter screen or more technical interviewer)

Label each section with timing. Flag which parts to rehearse most.

### 8. Questions to ask
8-10 strategic questions, organized by purpose:

- **Role clarity:** What does success look like at 30/90/180 days? What's broken that this role is meant to fix?
- **Stakeholder dynamics:** What are the biggest cross-functional friction points this role will need to navigate? How does the team make decisions when priorities conflict?
- **Culture signals:** How does leadership respond when a project hits a hard obstacle? What does "moving fast" mean here in practice?
- **Signal questions:** Questions that position me as already thinking strategically about the problem — not just asking about the job, but demonstrating how I'd approach it

Flag which questions are highest priority if time is short.

### 9. Salary positioning
- Market range for this role at this company type and location
- Where my target sits relative to that range and why
- Language to use if asked early in the process before I'm ready to anchor
- Language to use when I'm ready to anchor

### 10. Pre-interview checklist
What to do before the conversation:

- [ ] Outreach or networking moves (LinkedIn connection, referral ask, mutual contacts)
- [ ] Materials to update or tailor if not already done
- [ ] Practice priorities — which section of the ATEL needs the most rehearsal
- [ ] Logistics (time zone, format, who's calling whom, what to have ready)

Your Experience Deep Mine (or Career Memory Bank)
The single most useful thing you can do before working with AI on job applications is build a document that contains everything — not a polished version of everything, but the actual everything.

Call it whatever you want. I call mine the EDM.  I think the acronym originally came from Albano, who recommends creating an Experience Document Map for crafting your story.  For AI, and Experience Deep Mine makes more sense. You can find Albano here. https://www.linkedin.com/in/albano-gega/ - he presents an overall methodology for job seeking which you can evaluate for your use, separately.


What the EDM is 
Your resume is a curated surface. The Memory Bank is the full breadth of experience underneath it.  

A lot of online resume building tools will be better if they made you do the work of an EDM first.  When you're tailoring a resume or preparing for an interview, the AI can mine it for proof points you wouldn't have thought to reach for yourself.

How I built mine
Voice to text. I often used Audiopen to talk through career chapters as I paged through old notebooks, so I could capture a lot of detail easily, even if it didn’t seem relevant to the last role I saw.

The prompt I (informally) gave myself before each recording session: "What did I actually do, what was hard about it, who was involved, what changed because of it, and what would I want someone to know that wouldn't fit on a resume?"

Then structured it chronologically in a big ol'e google doc. I organized by company and year, but kept the raw notes intact. No cleaning up the rough edges — the rough edges are often where the best proof points live. Every so often, I add details about long-forgotten projects or details. I also added some past recommendations by peers; helpful for cover letters or to find descriptive lines that I feel good about saying.

What I generally included:

Role titles, dates, team sizes, and budget ownership at each stage
Project-level detail: what the problem was, what I built or changed, who I worked with, what the resistance was, what the outcome was
Metrics — including the uncertain ones, noted as uncertain ("~$14M in billing disputes resolved, verify with Ryan")
Vendor decisions: what I evaluated, what I chose, what I chose not to buy and why
Stakeholder dynamics: who the difficult partners were, how I navigated them
Negotiation plays and the thinking behind them
Team building: who I hired, how I developed them, what I learned when it didn't work
LinkedIn recommendations and colleague quotes, in full
Things that didn't make it onto the resume but happened — side projects, interim roles, moments where scope expanded
Early career detail that gets compressed in a standard resume but establishes technical credibility
Notes to self: things still to verify, gaps to fill, questions about how to frame something

What I did not do:

Polish it for an audience
Worry about length (mine is 31 pages, ~8,000 words)
Remove the uncertainty ("I think this number was X but need to confirm")
Cut the context that feels obvious to me — the AI doesn't have my context


Why it works
A resume is optimized for skimmability. A Memory Bank is optimized for retrieval.

When you ask an AI to tailor your resume for a specific role, the limiting factor is usually the quality of the source material. If all it has is your resume, it will rephrase your resume. If it has your Memory Bank, it can surface the Anaplan negotiation story when you're applying to a role that mentions financial systems, or the GDPR build when compliance architecture comes up, or the early Salesforce implementation at a small company when the role values scrappiness.


What to put in yours
The categories below are a starting point. Your career will have its own shape — follow that, not this list.

For each role:

Title, dates, reporting structure
Team size and composition (FTEs, contractors, consultants)
Budget owned (capex, opex, software spend)
The 3-5 things you spent most of your time on
Many things that you are proud of
What you built that didn't exist before
What you inherited and what you changed
Key stakeholders and how you worked with them, quotes or feedback from them

Projects:

What problem it solved and for whom
Scale (budget, timeline, number of systems or people affected)
Your specific contribution vs. the team's
What the resistance was and how you moved through it
Outcome — including uncertain metrics, noted as such

Vendors and platforms:

What you evaluated and selected
What you evaluated and didn't select, and why
Negotiation wins
Tools you retired

People:

Hires you're proud of and what made them work
Development moments — yours and theirs
Situations where you had to manage up, across, or through conflict

Recommendations and quotes:

LinkedIn recommendations in full
Informal quotes from colleagues or stakeholders
Awards, recognitions, nominations

Early career:

Don't compress this too aggressively. Technical credibility, domain exposure, and formative experiences often live here.

Things you're still figuring out how to say:

Include them anyway. Notes like "I'm not sure how to frame this" are useful prompts for the AI to help you work it out.


Keeping it current
Add to it after significant projects, after interviews (especially if a question surfaces a story you hadn't thought to tell), and after you get feedback — positive or negative — on how you're presenting yourself.

It doesn't need to be tidy. It just needs to be honest and dense.


Using it with AI
When starting a new application, share your Memory Bank with the AI alongside the job description. Ask it to identify which experiences are most relevant before you start tailoring anything. You'll often be pointed at things you wouldn't have led with.

The checklist in this repo references your Memory Bank throughout. That's the workflow: JD in one hand, Memory Bank in the other, AI in the middle doing the matching.



Mine started as a voice memo and a blank doc. Two years and 8,000 words later, it's the most useful career document I have — and it's not on my resume.
